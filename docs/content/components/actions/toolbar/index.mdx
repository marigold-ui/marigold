`<Toolbar>` gathers the controls that act on a view — a search field, a few filters, a handful of actions — into one region the keyboard treats as a single stop. Reach for it at the top of a list, table, or editor: anywhere a user needs more than a couple of controls to work with what sits below. Instead of tabbing through every field and button in turn, they land on the toolbar once and step across it with the arrow keys.

For two or three related buttons on their own, a [`<ButtonGroup>`](/components/actions/button-group) is enough. When the actions apply to a selection the user has made in a collection, reach for an [`<ActionBar>`](/components/collection/actionbar) instead — it appears with the selection and clears with it. A toolbar is for the controls that are always there.

import { ToolbarAnatomy } from './toolbar-anatomy';

## Anatomy

<ToolbarAnatomy />

- **Toolbar:** The region itself. It announces the controls as a group and lets the user move across them with the arrow keys.
- **Controls:** Everything the toolbar holds. Inputs the user reads or types into, like a search field or a filter, and actions they trigger, like buttons.
- **Group:** An optional cluster of related controls, announced as a unit so the row reads as a few groups instead of one long run.
- **Separator:** A quiet vertical line that sets one cluster of controls apart from the next.
- **More:** When the bar runs out of room, its collapsing actions (each a `<Toolbar.Action>`) fold into this single overflow menu, and return as space allows.

## Appearance

<AppearanceDemo component="Toolbar" />

<AppearanceTable component="Toolbar" />

A toolbar carries almost no styling of its own. It lays its controls out as one spaced row and otherwise stays out of the way. `size` sets the bar's density — the spacing between its controls — but it does not resize the controls themselves, so each field and button keeps the size you give it. Keep the toolbar quiet and let it sit beneath the surface's title and its primary action.

## Usage

A toolbar reads left to right as one thought: the things you look through on the left, the things you do on the right. Put the inputs a user reads or types into — search, filters — first, and the actions that operate on the view after them. Give the whole region a name so assistive technology can announce what it is for.

### Filtering and searching a list

The most common toolbar sits above a list or table and narrows what it shows: a search field, one or two filters, and a way to reach the rest.

<ComponentDemo name="toolbar-filters" />

Keep search first and the filters beside it, so the eye starts where the user's attention does. When there are more filters than fit comfortably, move the long tail behind an **All filters** button that opens a [`<Drawer>`](/components/overlay/drawer), rather than crowding the bar with every option at once.

### Grouping related controls

When a toolbar carries several kinds of action, cluster the related ones so the row reads as a few groups instead of a long, flat line. A `<Toolbar.Group>` ties a set of controls together and announces them as a unit, and a `<Toolbar.Separator>` draws a quiet line between one cluster and the next.

<ComponentDemo name="toolbar-groups" />

Give every group a name, since that name is what tells assistive technology where one cluster ends and the next begins. Separators are decorative, so don't lean on them alone to convey the grouping.

### Bringing different controls together

A toolbar is not just a row of buttons. Any single, focusable control belongs in it — a search field, a [`<Switch>`](/components/form/switch), a [`<Select>`](/components/form/select), a [`<Menu>`](/components/overlay/menu) — and each joins the same single tab stop, reachable with the arrow keys. Mix them as the view needs.

<ComponentDemo name="toolbar-mixed" />

Keep the controls a user reads or sets — search, filters, toggles — on the left, and the actions that operate on the view on the right. Only `<Toolbar.Action>`s collapse when space runs short; inputs, toggles and menus keep their place, since a stateful control or one that opens its own overlay can't fold into a menu item. Composite, arrow-key-driven controls — a `<RadioGroup>`, a `<TagGroup>` — are best wrapped in a `<Toolbar.Group>` so their own navigation reads as a single stop.

### Letting actions collapse when space runs short

A toolbar should never wrap onto a second line or scroll sideways. Wrap each collapsing action in a `<Toolbar.Action>`: when the bar runs out of room they fold in from the right, one at a time, into a single **More** menu — reappearing as the bar grows. A `<Toolbar.Action>` is a _description_ of an action, not a rendered button, and that is what lets the toolbar show it as a button in the bar or as an item in the menu, whichever fits.

<ComponentDemo name="toolbar-overflow" />

Only `<Toolbar.Action>`s collapse, and only from the right. Anything else in the bar — a search field, a filter, a separator, even a plain `<Button>` — keeps its place, so put the controls that must stay visible on the left and the collapsing actions last. Order the actions by how often they are used, because the least-used ones are the first to disappear. To keep an action always visible — a primary **Save**, say — use a plain `<Button>` rather than a `<Toolbar.Action>`.

### Accessible labels

Compact toolbars lean on icon-only actions, and an icon on its own has no text for assistive technology to read. Give a `<Toolbar.Action>` an `icon` and a `label` and the toolbar does the rest: it renders an icon-only button, sets the `label` as its `aria-label`, pairs it with a [`<Tooltip>`](/components/overlay/tooltip) so sighted users find the same label on hover or focus, and reuses that `label` if the action later collapses into the **More** menu.

<ComponentDemo name="toolbar-labels" />

The `label` is required for an icon action — it is the only text assistive technology has, so make it a short action phrase like `Download report`. Name the toolbar itself as well, and make the name specific when the toolbar repeats per row, like `Actions for {item.name}`.

<Callout title="Keep it accessible">
  A tooltip is not a replacement for an `aria-label`. Tooltips are not reliably
  reachable on touch devices or by every assistive technology, so the accessible
  name has to live on the control. See [Tooltip:
  Accessibility](/components/overlay/tooltip#accessibility).
</Callout>

### Keeping the toolbar in view

On a long, scrolling view it often helps to pin the toolbar to the top so its search and filters stay within reach. A toolbar brings no positioning of its own — it sits wherever you place it — so make it stick with the layout around it. When you do, leave room at the top of the scroll area so a control the user tabs to never ends up hidden behind the pinned bar.

## Alternative components

- [ButtonGroup](/components/actions/button-group): Use for a small cluster of related buttons on their own, without inputs alongside them.
- [ActionBar](/components/collection/actionbar): Use for actions that apply to a selection in a collection; it appears with the selection and clears with it.
- [ActionMenu](/components/overlay/action-menu): The overflow menu pattern. A toolbar reaches for it automatically as its own **More** menu.
- [Menu](/components/overlay/menu): Use for a standalone list of commands behind a single trigger, rather than a row of always-visible controls.
