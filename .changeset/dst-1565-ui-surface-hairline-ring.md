refa(DST-1565): draw the `ui-surface` border as a translucent outset hairline instead of the gradient `background-clip` trick

`ui-surface` previously faked its border by reserving a `1px solid transparent` border and showing a `border-box`-clipped background gradient (with a darken-toward-bottom bevel) through it. That hijacked the entire `background` property and forced `ui-state-error` to override the border via a `border-*` color.

The border is now a 1px Tailwind `ring` (routed through `--tw-ring-shadow`), so it composes into the same `box-shadow` chain as `shadow-elevation-*` without clobbering them. `background` is a plain fill again, and `ui-state-error` just swaps `--ui-border-color`. The `--ui-border-color` theming contract is unchanged.

Two deliberate properties of the new hairline:

- **Translucent.** The resting color is a new `--color-surface-border` token â€” the charcoal hue at low alpha (`oklch(from var(--color-charcoal-950) l c h / 0.1)`). It is the _decorative_ border of a surface: it still draws a line, but sits deliberately below the non-text-contrast threshold and carries no meaning, so it must not be used where an edge is an affordance â€” functional boundaries take the opaque `--color-border`. Because it is translucent, the edge composites over whatever ground the surface sits on, so its contrast stays consistent on white, the page background, or a tinted panel â€” where a fixed gray reads heavy on white and washes out on gray. State overrides (focus â†’ `--color-ring`, error â†’ `--color-destructive-accent`) remain opaque on purpose.
- **Outset.** The ring sits just outside the box, so it is painted above child content. A child that bleeds edge-to-edge (e.g. a `Table` inside `Panel.Content bleed`) can no longer cover the left/right border â€” which an inset ring or a `background-clip` border did.

Form controls keep a clearly visible boundary. A field edge is an affordance the user must see (WCAG 1.4.11 non-text contrast), so controls wear a dedicated `ui-surface-control` utility (see below) that swaps the `ui-surface` ring to the opaque `--color-border` â€” the same boundary color Checkbox and Radio already use â€” rather than the lighter translucent `--color-surface-border` rim. The decorative surface rim stays whisper-light for Card/Panel/Dialog/Menu, while every control boundary reads with one consistent opaque line.

The opaque `--color-border` token is darkened a notch in the same pass â€” from `charcoal-300` to `oklch(from var(--color-charcoal-300) calc(l - 0.08) c h)` (L 0.86 â†’ 0.78). At charcoal-300 the edge sat only ~6% darker than a white field and dissolved into it; the darker value reads as a deliberate control boundary. Honest caveat: at L 0.78 the line still measures only ~2:1 against white, short of WCAG 1.4.11's 3:1 non-text-contrast bar (clearing 3:1 would need ~L 0.67, a visibly heavier line than the design wants). The lighter weight is a deliberate design call; the gap is documented inline on the token and tracked separately. Because every control border (inputs, selects, date fields, checkboxes, radios) already shares this single token, they all darken consistently in one move. The darkening is scoped to the `--color-border` token rather than the `charcoal-300` rung on purpose â€” moving the rung would also drag scrollbars and the date-segment focus highlight and compress the 200/300/400 ramp. The token is still derived from `charcoal-300` so its lineage to the scale is intact. It continues to be used for grid lines where a translucent stroke would double-darken at intersections (`Table` rows/columns), which sit on white and have no ground to adapt to.

Content separators that are a light visual hint rather than a functional affordance â€” `SelectList` row dividers, the standalone `Divider`, and `Menu` section rules â€” move off the opaque `--color-border` onto the decorative `--color-surface-border` hairline, so a boxed list reads as one consistent weight with its surface ring instead of showing internal lines heavier than the frame around them. Because the selected-row fill (`--color-selected`) is close in tone to that faint hairline, a `SelectList` divider that touches a selected row â€” its own edge and the divider on the row directly before it â€” darkens back to the opaque `--color-border` so both edges of the selection stay crisp, wherever it lands; dividers between unselected rows stay faint. A hovered row keeps its faint separators. This is scoped to the `default` variant (the `bordered` variant already gives a selected item its own edge). `Table` grid lines stay on `--color-border` because crossing translucent strokes would double-darken at intersections.

Because `--ui-border-color` is registered `inherits: false`, the opaque-border contract only reaches the ring when `ui-input` sits on the same element as the `ui-surface` ring. That holds for the single-element controls (Input, Select, TextArea, TagField), but the composite controls split the two across elements â€” `DateField` and `NumberField` put `ui-surface` on the field/group wrapper and `ui-input` on the inner input â€” so the var never reached the ring and those controls fell back to the lighter translucent `--color-surface-border` rim (e.g. a `DatePicker`'s field read visibly lighter than the inputs beside it). Both now use `ui-surface-control` on their wrapper (which owns the opaque boundary directly), so every control boundary matches. Their focused-invalid border was also aligned to `--color-destructive-accent` (matching `ui-state-error`) instead of `--color-destructive`.

Visually, for _surfaces_ the old 1px border gradient/bevel is dropped (a flat ring cannot gradient), the resting edge is a touch softer (translucent vs. opaque charcoal-300), and corners antialias slightly differently. _Controls_ revive the gradient edge through `ui-surface-control` (below).

To keep the distinction between decorative surfaces and interactive controls legible now that the surface hairline is so soft, controls wear a dedicated `ui-surface-control` utility instead of `ui-surface`. It builds on `ui-surface` but (1) swaps the ring to the opaque `--color-border`, and (2) darkens the bottom edge of that border one step via a crisp 1px inset line â€” so the boundary reads lighter-top / darker-bottom (reviving the old gradient border) while the fill stays perfectly flat; the gradient lives on the border, not the surface. The line derives from `--ui-border-color`, so focus/error recolors carry into it and `ui-state-disabled` (`inset-shadow-none`) removes it. Elevation stays a separate `shadow-elevation-border` so the boundary and the lift compose independently. Applied across the control family â€” Input, Select, TextArea, NumberField, DateField, TagField, the secondary Button, Menu's neutral trigger, ToggleButton, Tag, SelectList, and a standalone ListBox â€” while a ListBox/Menu/Calendar inside a Popover or Tray stays flat (the overlay frame draws the surface). Cards, Panels, Dialogs and other true surfaces keep the soft `ui-surface` hairline.

`ui-surface-contrast` (primary Button, ActionBar) is reworked to the same model, inverted in value. The destructive Button wears a thin `ui-surface-destructive` wrapper over it â€” pointing `--ui-background-color` at `--color-destructive-bold` retints every layer (glow, top-down fill, top-edge highlight, dark ring) red, reviving the light gradient it carried before while sharing one mechanism with the primary Button. It dropped the `background-clip` border and its lighter-than-fill edge â€” which ringed the dark surface in light â€” for a 1px `ring` that is a darker shade of the fill (a crisp dark hairline, no rim). The light now lives on the face: a soft glow pooled at the top that fades before the edges, over a gentle top-down fill gradient, plus a soft 1px highlight along the very top edge (a warm-tinted, low-alpha `inset-shadow` â€” a catch of light, not a bright rim). Ring, highlight, glow, and gradient all derive from the `--ui-highlight-color` / `--ui-background-color` override vars, so themes can retint.

The three elevation tokens (`--shadow-elevation-border/raised/overlay`) are retuned to match. Now that the ring owns the crisp edge, the shadows carry only lift: the harsh contact layer (previously up to `0.32` alpha) is cut sharply and the darkness spread across lighter layers, lit from above with negative spread so each shadow stays contained inside its own footprint â€” a dense field of elevated elements stays calm instead of bleeding into a haze. The shadow color is tinted to the warm charcoal hue instead of pure black so the lift sits into the warm-neutral ground.
